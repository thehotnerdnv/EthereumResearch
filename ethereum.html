About Ethereum

Ethereum is a type of block chain technology that is transforming the way we think of decentralized systems and applications. Different to bitcoin, Which was primary developed as a digital currency, Ethereum was developed as a platform for smart contracts and decentralised apps (dApps) . It has adaptability that has put it in the forefront of both the blockchain and cryptocurrency world.

Origins of Ethereum

In 2013, Vitalik Buterin proposed Ethereum while he was a teenager. However, his idea of the platform was more capable than Bitcoin which sparked revolution in blockchain technology. He wanted a blockchain system that would run code and automate agreements without any intermediaries. In 2014 development of the Ethereum network began and formally launched on the 30 July ,2015. This platform featured a programmable blockchain which enabled developers to deploy and create their own decentralized apps(Buterin, 2013; Ethereum Foundation,2020).

Key Features of Ethereum

Ethereum's defining characteristic is its capability for smart contracts. Smart contracts are self-executing contracts in which the terms of the agreement are directly written in code. They are built with Solidity, a programming language intended exclusively for Ethereum (Ethereum Foundation, 2020). For example, a smart contract may automatically transmit cash once a service is done without the need for a third party, eliminating the requirement for parties to trust each other.

Smart contracts have resulted in the development of Decentralized Finance (DeFi) applications that seek to replicate traditional financial services in a decentralized fashion. These services are based on blockchain technology and include lending, borrowing, and trading platforms like as Aave and Compound (Narayanan et al. 2016). DeFi has democratized access to financial services, making them more accessible and transparent for people worldwide (Buterin, 2018).

Ethereum’s Cryptocurrency: Ether

Ethereum also has its own coin, Ether (ETH). On the Ethereum network, ether is used for a variety of purposes, including paying for transaction fees, computational services, and incentivizing network users. The fee scheme in Ethereum is called as "gas," and it varies dependent on network demand. Ether has emerged as a popular cryptocurrency in its own right, serving as a store of wealth, medium of trade, and investment vehicle (CoinMarketCap, 2020).

Ether has also become a popular cryptocurrency in its own right, used as a store of value, a medium of exchange, and an investment. It’s the second-largest cryptocurrency by market capitalization, second only to Bitcoin.

Applications of Ethereum

Ether has also become a popular cryptocurrency in its own right, used as a store of value, a medium of exchange, and an investment. It’s the second-largest cryptocurrency by market capitalization, second only to Bitcoin.

DeFi applications on Ethereum include lending and borrowing platforms, decentralized exchanges, and stablecoins. For instance, platforms like Aave and Compound allow users to lend their cryptocurrency to others and earn interest, all managed through smart contracts. This has democratized access to financial services, especially for people in regions where traditional banking infrastructure is limited.

Another major application of Ethereum is in the creation of Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs). NFTs are unique digital assets that represent ownership of a specific item or piece of content, such as art, music, videos, or virtual real estate. Unlike cryptocurrencies, which are interchangeable (or fungible), NFTs are one-of-a-kind. The rise of NFTs has opened new opportunities for creators, allowing them to monetize their work in innovative ways. Ethereum is the primary blockchain used for minting and trading NFTs, making it a hub for digital art and collectibles.

Challenges Faced by Ethereum

Despite its many strengths, Ethereum has faced several challenges. One of the biggest issues is scalability. The current Ethereum network can process only about 15 transactions per second, which is significantly lower than centralized systems like Visa. This limitation has led to network congestion and high gas fees during periods of high demand.

Another challenge is energy consumption. Ethereum, like Bitcoin, originally used a Proof-of-Work (PoW) consensus mechanism, which requires significant computational power to validate transactions and secure the network. This has raised concerns about the environmental impact of Ethereum’s operations.

Ethereum 2.0: The Future of Ethereum

To address these challenges, Ethereum is undergoing a major upgrade known as Ethereum 2.0 or ETH 2.0. This upgrade involves transitioning from a Proof-of-Work (PoW) to a Proof-of-Stake (PoS) consensus mechanism. Under PoS, validators are chosen to verify transactions based on the amount of Ether they hold and are willing to "stake" as collateral. This method is far more energy-efficient and scalable.

Ethereum 2.0 also introduces sharding, a technique that splits the blockchain into smaller, more manageable pieces called shards. Sharding will significantly increase the network's capacity, enabling it to process thousands of transactions per second. These upgrades aim to make Ethereum more sustainable, secure, and user-friendly, ensuring its long-term relevance in the blockchain ecosystem.

The Impact of Ethereum

Ethereum has had a profound impact on the blockchain and cryptocurrency space. Its introduction of programmable smart contracts has inspired countless other blockchain projects and platforms. It has also paved the way for a more decentralized internet, often referred to as Web3. Web3 envisions a future where users have greater control over their data and online interactions, with Ethereum serving as a foundational layer for this vision.

Moreover, Ethereum has fostered a vibrant developer community. Thousands of developers around the world are building dApps on Ethereum, contributing to its growth and innovation. From gaming and healthcare to real estate and governance, Ethereum's applications are as diverse as they are transformative.
